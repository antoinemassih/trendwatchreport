The diagram above is not a metaphor. It is a competency inventory. A production-grade web application, SaaS product, or enterprise platform requires engineering work across all thirteen layers. The vibe-coded prototype addresses one. The other twelve are not faster to build because the first came quickly. They exist entirely outside the scope of what the generative tooling touches.

The mechanism that makes this new is not the prototype itself — prototypes have always been incomplete. The mechanism is the removal of the warning signal. A grey-box Figma mockup communicated “this is not built.” A developer-rendered HTML sketch communicated “this is not production.” A Lovable build communicates nothing of the sort. It loads. It animates. It accepts input. It looks finished. And the twelve engineering layers it has not touched are entirely invisible to anyone who does not already know they exist.

The consequence is not a technical problem. It is an expectation problem at the point of commissioning. Clients who see the prototype assume the hardest work is done. They are not wrong to assume this — they have been given no information to the contrary. What they have seen is, objectively, the most visually impressive part of the product. The problem is that visual impressiveness and production readiness are now structurally decoupled in a way they never were before.

The prototype now looks like the product. The distance between them has never been larger.

06  ·  NIM’s Take

The prototype proved the concept.
Now build the product.

Here is what breaks first: auth. In almost every vibe-coded system that reaches NIM after a failed launch, the authentication layer — or the RLS policies, or both — is either absent or incorrectly configured. It is invisible in the prototype. It is catastrophic in production. NIM’s diagnostic does not begin with the frontend, which is almost always the strongest part of what was built. It begins with Layer 04 and Layer 08, because that is where the exposure lives.

The second thing that breaks is scope. The client has seen the frontend and estimated the project is eighty percent complete. The production engineering work has not been scoped, priced, or discussed. When the true architecture becomes visible — when the 12 layers that did not exist in the prototype become line items in an engineering proposal — the project is already committed to a timeline and budget that cannot accommodate them. The scope conversation that was skipped at week one is now a renegotiation at week eight, under pressure, after relationships have been strained.

The third thing — the one that causes the most damage in the long run — is the archaeology problem. When production engineers inherit a vibe-coded codebase, they find no documentation of intent. No commit messages that reflect architectural decisions. No README that explains why the database schema is shaped the way it is, or why a particular API was structured against convention. They must reverse engineer not just the code, but the reasoning behind it, before they can safely extend or scale any part of the system. This is not a refactoring problem. It is a knowledge transfer problem — and it is structural to how AI-generated code is produced.

The prototype is not the enemy. Used correctly, it is the fastest validation tool the industry has ever produced. The fallacy is the belief that validating a concept at Layer 01 also validates the other twelve layers. NIM’s starting point with any vibe-coded system is a production readiness audit: what was built, what was not built, and what breaks first when external users arrive.

07  ·  Strategy
01

Run the 13-layer scope review before the project starts

Before any vibe-coded build begins, walk through all thirteen production layers with the client and identify which ones are in scope for the prototype phase, which are planned for the production build, and which have not been discussed. Make this a deliverable, not a conversation. A completed scope matrix at the start of a project is the single most effective intervention for preventing the expectation gap. It establishes what the prototype proves and what it does not address.

Effort: Low Day 1
02

Run a security scan before the first external user

Before any vibe-coded prototype reaches an external audience — users, investors, beta testers — run Semgrep or an equivalent SAST tool against the codebase. The scan takes minutes. The output is the honest inventory of what the generative tooling missed: exposed keys, missing authentication middleware, absent RLS policies. This is not a security solution. It is a gap audit. But it prevents the most common failure mode in vibe-coded production launches: exposure through the invisible layers.

Effort: Low 1 Day
03

Bring production engineers into the project at week two, not month six

The most expensive mistake in vibe-coded project timelines is treating production engineering as a later-stage concern. By the time the prototype is client-approved and the production engineering scope becomes visible, the project is locked into a timeline and budget that cannot accommodate the actual work. A production architect involved at week two can identify the infrastructure decisions that constrain everything downstream — database schema, auth model, cloud architecture — before those decisions are baked into the codebase.

Effort: Medium Week 2
04

Document architectural decisions as you build, not after

The archaeology problem is not solvable retroactively. The only intervention is prospective: document the why of every significant architectural decision at the point it is made. Why was this database schema chosen? Why is this API structured this way? Why was this authentication flow preferred over the alternative? An AI-generated codebase documents the what automatically. The why requires human intention. Without it, the codebase is a system no engineer can safely extend without rebuilding their understanding of it from scratch.

Effort: Low Ongoing
05

Define “done” as production-ready, not prototype-complete

Establish a project completion definition that includes all thirteen production layers before the prototype is presented to the client. The prototype demonstrates concept validity. Production readiness requires a signed-off definition that includes: security audit completed, CI/CD pipeline live, staging environment operational, error tracking instrumented, backup and recovery tested, RLS policies reviewed. The goal is not to expand scope. It is to make scope legible at the point when it can still be discussed without conflict.

Effort: Low Week 1
06

Use the prototype for what it was designed for: validation

The vibe-coded prototype is the fastest validation tool the industry has ever produced. It is not fast software. It is fast proof. The correct use of Lovable, Cursor, Claude Design, and equivalent tools is to compress the time from idea to validated concept — before committing engineering resources to production architecture. When this sequence is followed, the tools perform exactly as designed. When the prototype is handed directly to a production team and described as the product, the fallacy begins.

The New Economics of AI

AI just got 97% cheaper. Now it’s costing more than ever. Understanding how that’s possible — and what to do about it — is the foundation of every AI investment that actually works.

97%
Drop in the cost of AI intelligence since 2023
a16z · Andreessen Horowitz 2025
01  ·  Landscape

Two years ago, running a single AI conversation cost several cents. Today that same conversation costs a fraction of a cent. The price of AI intelligence has collapsed faster than almost any technology in history — dropping by 97% between 2023 and 2025.

This is genuinely good news. What was once accessible only to companies with deep technical budgets is now available to any business willing to experiment. A small team can run hundreds of thousands of AI interactions a month for the cost of a business lunch.

But the economics have a second side that most companies don’t see until the quarterly bill arrives. As AI gets cheaper per use, companies use dramatically more of it. New workflows get built. Old ones get upgraded. Experiments multiply. The total spend rises even as the per-unit cost falls — and unlike a SaaS subscription, AI charges for every single action, every time.

The companies thriving in this environment treat AI costs the same way mature technology companies treat cloud costs: with dedicated governance, routing logic, usage budgets, and attribution. The companies struggling are the ones who signed up for AI as if it were a flat monthly fee and then discovered it behaves more like electricity.

Uber deployed an AI agent for internal operations and within four months it had burned through its entire annual token budget. The reason wasn’t a flaw in the agent — it was how agents fundamentally work.

An AI agent doesn’t make one API call. It thinks in loops. Each reasoning step, each tool it uses, each decision it reconsiders is a separate API call. A task that looks like a single conversation from the outside might be 50 to 200 API calls under the hood. At scale, this compounds fast.

↳  The Access Paradox

The most capable AI models — the ones that can write strategy, analyse complex documents, or build sophisticated code — cost 10 to 50 times more per use than their lightweight alternatives. This creates an uncomfortable question for every AI budget: who should have access to the expensive models?

OpenAI’s own research found a 6× productivity gap between the top 10% of AI users and the median user given identical access to the same tools. The top users have developed skills and workflows that make the model dramatically more effective. The median user, given premium access without training, often uses it for tasks the cheap model handles just as well.

“Giving a $50/hour AI model to someone who uses it for spell-checking is like hiring a senior consultant to type emails. The economics are catastrophic at scale.”

↳  The FinOps Response

AI FinOps — governing AI spend the same way cloud FinOps governs infrastructure costs — is becoming standard practice inside mature AI organisations. It’s not about being cheap. It’s about building the cost infrastructure that lets you scale AI responsibly without a finance team shutting down programmes in the Q4 review.

The four controls that make the difference: smart routing (match the model to the task), semantic caching (return the saved answer for repeated queries), token budgets (hard caps per user, team, or workflow), and audit trails (every dollar attributed to a business function).

Brands that entered this era with a documented point of view, a defined voice, and a clear understanding of what they want their audience to feel now hold an asymmetric advantage. The Claim

“We finally have production parity. A $500-a-month stack does what a $200,000 agency retainer used to. For the first time, a challenger brand can compete on content volume with category leaders.”

The argument is legitimate and widely held. eMarketer’s 2025 AI marketing data shows the fastest adoption of AI video tools among mid-market brands — precisely because the tools close a production gap that previously required agency spend they couldn’t justify. The Counter

“Production parity is the floor, not the ceiling. When every brand produces at the same technical quality, the differentiator is no longer production — it’s story. The brands winning aren’t the ones producing more. They’re the ones producing with a point of view.”

Production quality was never the reason certain brands commanded attention and loyalty. It was a proxy for investment and seriousness. Once that proxy is universally accessible — and Gemini Omni has now put it in the hands of anyone with a $20 Google subscription — the signal disappears entirely. What replaces it is not a new production signal. It is the absence of any signal at all. Feeds filled with technically proficient AI content, all generated by the same tools, all exhibiting the same aesthetic signatures, are precisely what audiences are learning to scroll past. The brands that will hold attention are not the ones producing more. They are the ones producing with a point of view the tools cannot supply.

The Claim is correct — and that is precisely why it changes nothing. When the capability is universal, differentiation returns to its source: the clarity of what a brand has to say, and the discipline with which it chooses to say it.

Pillar 07  ·  The New Deliverable

Code Is the New Content

When the cost of producing something collapses, it stops being a deliverable and becomes a floor. Code is the latest craft to cross that line — and the value, like the work, is moving somewhere else.

01  ·  Landscape

There is a pattern that repeats every time a profession gets a powerful new tool. Desktop publishing put typesetting on every desk, and within a decade a professional website stopped being a specialist commission and became the baseline thing every designer was expected to deliver. The premium of one era quietly became the floor of the next. The assumption in most boardrooms today is that AI is the next turn of that handle — that it simply lets the same people ship more sites, more apps, more content, faster and cheaper.

That assumption is half right, and the half it gets wrong is the expensive half. What is collapsing is not the time it takes to do the work. It is the price of the artifact itself. A functional interface, a working prototype, a block of code — the things that used to be the proof of professional skill — can now be generated by anyone, in minutes, at almost no cost. When the cost of producing something falls to nearly zero, that thing stops being a deliverable you can charge for and becomes a commodity everyone already has.

This is what it means to say code is the new content. A decade ago, brands discovered that articles and videos — once scarce, commissioned, expensive — had become cheap and infinite, and the value migrated away from making content toward judgment, distribution, and taste — the writer who could merely produce copy lost ground; the one who decided what was worth saying gained it. Software is now crossing the same threshold. The artifact is becoming the floor, not the offer. The strategic question is no longer how much you can produce. It is what you are producing that the machine cannot.

The companies that read this correctly are not racing to ship more. They are working out which of their deliverables have just been commoditised, and rebuilding their offer — and their teams — around the layer that sits above the flood. The ones that misread it are scaling production of the exact thing the market is about to stop paying for.

The lesson is uncomfortable for anyone selling speed today: democratisation drops the average quality first, and lifts it later — and the gap between those two moments is exactly where the market is now.

As the floor rises, the work does not stay evenly distributed. The danger isn’t that AI replaces the designer or the developer. It’s that it quietly deletes the middle of the career — the rung where competent production used to be a living, while the top and the floor both survive.

Pillar 08  ·  The Taste Problem

When Everything Is Competent

AI has made competence infinite and nearly free. The moment everyone can produce work that is good, “good” stops being worth anything — and the only defensible position left is being distinctly, unmistakably yours.

01  ·  Landscape

For most of the modern economy, being good at something was a moat. Competence — a clean piece of writing, a well-built interface, a sound analysis — was scarce because it took years to acquire and time to produce. Buyers paid for it precisely because not everyone could deliver it. That is the assumption most organisations still price and hire against: that competent output is a differentiator worth paying for.

Generative AI has quietly dissolved that assumption. The machine’s great talent is competence at infinite scale: passable copy, serviceable design, working code, reasonable strategy — all of it, instantly, for almost nothing. And there is rigorous evidence this comes with a hidden cost. A controlled study in Science Advances found that AI makes each individual’s output measurably better while making everyone’s output measurably more alike. The floor rises and the work converges at the same time.

This is the taste problem. When competence is free and abundant, it becomes the baseline, not the offer — and the scarce, defensible quality moves to the one thing the machine flattens: distinctiveness. The culture has already named the failure state. In 2025 dictionaries chose “slop” as their word of the year, for the tide of cheap, competent, indistinguishable AI output now filling every channel.

Here is the subtlety most miss. Taste is not magic, and it is not un-systematisable — you can document standards, build rubrics, and train a model on your preferences, and quality will genuinely improve. But systematised taste produces competence. What it cannot produce is the living judgment that makes work feel irreplaceably like one organisation rather than like “premium.” In an era of infinite competent output, that judgment layer is the only thing that does not commoditise.

↳  The Limit of Systematising Taste

The way machines learn taste is through reward models — scalable proxies for human judgment, trained on examples of what people prefer. They work. They are exactly how a model is taught to be more helpful, more on-brand, more competent. This is the strongest version of “taste can be systematised,” and it is true.

But it has a structural ceiling. Optimising toward a single reward function compresses the diversity of human preference into one average standard. The research on this is explicit: aligning to one reward ignores the spread of what people actually value. You get reliable competence and you lose the outlier — and the outlier is where distinctiveness lives.

A system can be taught your standards, and it will reliably produce work that is good. It cannot be taught to produce the work that is unmistakably yours — because that lives in the judgment you haven’t written down yet.

The system reliably produces “good.” The exception — the thing that makes work unmistakably yours — is exactly what optimisation erases. You can scale competence. You cannot scale a signature.

Both are right, and that is the whole point. Systematise taste and you will reliably reach competence — the new table stakes. Distinctiveness needs a living human standard held above the system, deciding when to break the rule the system would keep.
